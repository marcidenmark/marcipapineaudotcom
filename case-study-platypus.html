Platypus
Startup working with human resource software
Business Case

Platypus is a Copenhagen based startup working within HR. Its focus is to help companies make good hires, and retain talent.

The Challenge

Platypus was in an early stage of development. They had a low tech prototype that was successfully used to secure a round of pre-seed funding and early stage users.

This next iteration should greatly enhance the experience for early stage users with an easy to use interface, and continue to get new companies to buy into the product.

There are 2 core parts of the product- an in depth user profile completion process, and a dashboard for onboarded companies to utilize the information collected from their users.

Project Scope & Objectives

With a 3 month deadline the product needed a range of functions.

» A user creation interface that would work on mobile and desktop

» A multi screen dashboard displaying the data gathered during the user onboarding process

» A more robust working product with solid UX/UI

» Improved usability of product

» Less clicks and more condensed information presented on fewer screens

» An inhanced way to visualize the data– the core aspect of the product

» A replacement to sending information to users via a PDF attached to an email

» Keep existing clients happy and excited for upcoming features

» Continue to help selling the product to new companies

» Bring in more funding

The Outcome

✓ A new round of funding of over 1 million kroner

✓ A signup flow including multiple levels of information that worked on mobile and desktop

✓ Entire new way to filter– eliminating the need for complicated dropdown menus

✓ CSS broken into modular components and properties for easy reusability

✓ CSS Grid coded for easy completion of all future layouts

✓ Coded HTML emails used during the signup process (instead of PDFs) – based on investors feedback about it being difficult to get people to read and complete their emails.

The Process

Weekly Product Meetings Once a week we met with the entire team to review what we were woring on, and voice opinions. We spoke about features in progress, the weeks features to be worked on, and features we wanted to pursue, but not in the upcoming week. These were all put into a Trello board to help keep track of all of it.

Dashboard research Tons of reviews of existing dashboard layouts for inspiration. Together with the UXer, we looked at many different dashboards. Some were for products that were similar to ours, and others that were not, but had good ways to visualize information.

On the left is the wireframe covering the foundation for the page. Upon refinement, it was used to write the copy for the site.

UI Research I constantly looked for inspiration for great UI design. One of the places I often go to is Steve Schoger's book, "Refactoring UI." Some of what was particularly helpful was his visualizations of typographical hierarchy principles. [ If you're curious, you can see a few of his tips here ]

New way to show information instead of dropdown menus Upon starting the project there were many dropdown menus that were hard to navigate. I knew it would be good with an option, and during research came across "Improving the usability of multi-selecting from a long list" on Medium. It even had data showing longer retention by using other options. [ Read Research Article, "Improving the usability of multi-selecting from a long list" ]

ABOVE: A sample of cards in the dashboard.

Graphic Research There was a JS library already being used. I looked into different options for visualizing what we needed.

Coding Research Looked into the different ways the layout could be coded. One of the things I know from the start was that I wanted to code a grid that could easily be used throughout the product. One of the considerations was Flexbox? Grid? Grid won.

Coding Built pages and screens writting HTML & CSS– working closely with the backend developer and UXer.

Data Visualization Research Looked into how to show the data so it could be easily understood. Part of the considerations involved different libraries to use.

Coding Emails I had not done this before. But found it really important to get away from sending information via pdfs in an email. Emails can be tricky because they need to be written in an extremely old type of html (using tables). Plus, there is a lot of standard CSS that doesn't work.

Typography & Colors Were supplied from the UXer.

Rational for Design Solutions

Desktop First Dashboard Wait, what did I just say?! I saw Søren Engelbrecht, Senior Mobile Strategist at A.P. Moller - Maersk give a talk, "Mobile When Necessary." This shattered my idea of mobile first ALWAYS. Søren's story spoke of Mærsk's large shipping company and their digital needs. Some of work needs to be caried out while working on a multiple large screens with multiple levels of complicated information. They will never be able to do this on their cell phones on the way home from work. So NO to mobile design. But, other things are done almost entirely on a small device while workers are out on the docks. So mobile first, and maybe even mobile only. It makes perfect sence of course.

Research with the Platypus founders, who have years of experience working in HR, said that they didn't use their phones for complicated hiring work, as there was too much information you needed to see at once, in order for it to make sence. So, with this in mind, we went toward the desktop application first (for the dashboards) and "mobile when necessary."

Mobile First Signup and User Onboarding Interface On the other hand, when users are signing up for the product, they are often NOT at a computer screen. So phones were the highest priority. The design and code was written for a mobile screen first, and then optimized for desktop.
