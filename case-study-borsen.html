Børsen
Business and finance newspaper headquartered in Copenhagen, Denmark
Business Case

Børsen is read by industry leaders, and used to make actionable decisions. Their need was to improve the usability of their information graphics by making them easier to understand in a shorter amount of time.

The Challenge

Børsen's readers are busy. They don't have a lot of time to read the paper, yet it is an important part of their day. Information needs to be understood quickly. Børsen has a lot of information graphics, that help tell a story of a thousand words in five to ten seconds.

The graphics at the time were difficult to understand. They were represented in units that didn't match, units of measure were hard to find and, graphic forms were not always make in the correct form. The story was confused.

Marci has sent Børsen on a most needed journey to get infographics in the newspaper that the readers can actually read and understand.
Jørgen Andresen Head of Media at Dagbladet Børsen
Project Scope & Objectives

A review of the process of how graphics were being created from start to end was to be made so that there could be new guidelines on how to create a usable graphic that was easy to understand.

The ultimate goal was to find a way to make sure the graphic was written and designed with information that made sense. And followed a set of rules for creating the graphic in a consistent design style.

The Solution

After evaluations and meetings with top management, created guidelines and processes to aid in the creation of a graphic– from the starting stage of a writer requesting a graphic to it being proofread before publication.

✓ Improved communication and working processes between art and editorial departments

✓ Reworked paper’s information graphics and created style guide.

✓ Laid out new rules and guidelines on how to:

1. Create a good information graphic.

2. Choose the correct graphic form to communicate the core message.

3. Present graphics that are quick and easy to understand.

✓ Use typography and line styles and weights to communicate multiple levels of information.

My Roll

I spent the first days watching the work of the graphics department and how they interacted with the other parts of the paper. I reviewed the contents of the graphics, and made notes on where improvements were needed. I held meetings with the senior levels of art and editorial departments to discuss my findings. I then worked on creating the new guideline– both visually and editorially. There were meetings back and forth to discuss progress. The project was completed my my delivery of a detailed style guide and Illustrator files that could be used as a skeleton.

New kinds of graphics

The project ended with new ways to visualize data at the end. See here for a few examples.

Above right (after): a new way to tell a story
The older graphic (BEFORE) was eye catching but the story wasn't explained well.
It showed there had been some ups and downs– ending high, but the fact that the end of the graphic is only a projection is not apparent.

The graphic on the right (AFTER) is both quick and easy to understand.
There is a key on the bottom telling the quantity of one circle, and you get the answer to the story in a few seconds. If you are excited by the graphic you would dive into the story.
Bubble Graphic
The bubble graphic is a great way to show information because it gives the option of multiple levels of data than can be in a simple graph. There is the x axis, y axis, colors and size to be utilized.
Lollipop Chart
This lollipop chart adds a 3rd dimension by using the size of the circle to add another level of infomation to the chart. It can be a more compelling than a traditional bar chart.
